## Who should own a ChatGPT Business rollout?

Most credible UK rollouts name a specific owner in week one — typically someone in IT or operations — plus a leadership sponsor who can unblock decisions and approve expansion. Without a named owner, workspace configuration, policy decisions, and pilot user selection tend to drift between departments, and the rollout stalls before it produces anything measurable. The owner does not need to be technical; they need authority to approve pilot workflows and enough time allocated to actually run the pilot rather than treat it as a side project.

The leadership sponsor's job is narrower but just as important: removing friction. When a pilot user asks whether a workflow is approved, or a department wants to expand seats faster than planned, the sponsor is who signs off — without that role, minor decisions queue up and slow the rollout down regardless of how well the technical setup went.

## How do we measure adoption after go-live?

Weekly usage visibility inside the workspace is the starting point — active users, which workflows people actually run, and where pilot users hit friction. Pair that with a short qualitative check-in: are pilot users still using personal ChatGPT alongside Business, and if so, why. That combination catches the two most common failure signs early — licences nobody uses, and a governed workspace that has not actually displaced the shadow AI behaviour it was meant to replace.

Expansion decisions should follow the same evidence rather than a fixed calendar date. If pilot metrics show safe, useful patterns after two to four weeks, that is the signal to expand seats — not an arbitrary "month two" deadline that ignores whether the pilot actually worked.
