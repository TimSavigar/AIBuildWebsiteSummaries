**In brief:** Licences without rollout become shelfware. Buying seats does not fix shadow AI or produce measurable outcomes on its own — teams need a named owner, approved workflows, training, and expansion criteria. Rollout deliverables in the first month typically include a workspace owner and leadership sponsor, pilot users with three starter workflows, a published AI use policy and data handling red lines, and weekly adoption metrics with expansion criteria defined upfront.

This guide is for IT project leads, operations managers, and business sponsors actually running the deployment — not a strategic overview of why ChatGPT Business matters, but the tactical decisions that determine whether a pilot succeeds: which three workflows to start with, what governance actually needs to exist at pilot stage, and what to measure so you know whether it worked.

## How long does rollout take?

Two to six weeks for many UK SMEs — depending on pilot size, policy work, and stakeholder availability. The range exists because the constraint is rarely the technical setup; it is how quickly a workspace owner and leadership sponsor can be named and given time to actually run the pilot. Organisations that name both roles in the first few days routinely land at the shorter end of that range; organisations still debating ownership in week three routinely land at the longer end, or stall entirely.

## Who should own the rollout?

Typically IT or operations with a leadership sponsor. They manage members, workflows, and measurement. The owner does not need deep AI expertise — they need authority to approve pilot workflows and enough dedicated time to run the pilot properly rather than fit it around other priorities.

## What does week one actually look like?

Week one is administrative, not technical — the ChatGPT Business workspace itself takes minutes to provision. The real work is naming the owner and sponsor, confirming who the first five to ten pilot users will be, and drafting the one-page data red lines document before anyone gets access. Teams that skip this and provision access first tend to end up with pilot users experimenting randomly rather than testing the three workflows that actually matter, which makes the pilot data much harder to interpret at the end.

By the end of week one, a well-run rollout has: a named owner and sponsor, pilot users identified and invited, workspace access configured with admin controls set, and the three starter workflows written down with a human review gate defined for each. Weeks two through four are then about running those workflows for real and watching what pilot users actually do with them.

## How do we avoid the most common rollout delay?

The most common delay is not technical — it is indecision about who owns the rollout, or a sponsor who is named on paper but does not actually make time to unblock decisions. The second most common delay is choosing workflows based on what looks impressive in a demo rather than what pilot users will use every day. Both are avoidable by settling ownership and workflow choice before provisioning any access, rather than working them out mid-pilot while users are already waiting.

## How do we choose which three workflows to pilot first?

Pick workflows that are high-volume, low-risk, and already happening informally — drafting, research summaries, and internal knowledge tasks are common starting points. Avoid regulated or client-facing outputs until pilot users have built confidence and a human review gate is proven to work. The goal is a workflow pilot users will actually use daily, not a showcase demo nobody repeats.

A useful test: if you removed ChatGPT Business tomorrow, would pilot users notice within a day? If the answer is no, the workflow was not embedded in real work and will not survive past the pilot regardless of how well the demo went.

## What counts as "basic governance" during a pilot?

Pilot-stage governance does not need a full AI use policy — it needs a one-page set of data red lines, a named workspace owner, and a human review gate on each of the three pilot workflows. A more complete policy comes later, once real usage shows what actually needs governing; see the governance guide for the fuller policy checklist once you are past the pilot stage.

## What should we measure during the pilot?

Weekly active users against your pilot cohort, which of the three approved workflows people actually use, and whether pilot users still fall back to personal ChatGPT. Those three numbers tell you more than a satisfaction survey — usage data shows what actually happened, while a survey mostly shows what people think they should say.

## What happens if the pilot doesn't go well?

Treat a slow pilot as data, not failure — check whether the chosen workflows solve a real daily problem, whether training covered practical prompts rather than generic features, and whether the workspace owner has enough time allocated. Most stalled pilots trace back to one of those three gaps rather than the tool itself. Fixing the gap and re-running a short pilot is usually faster than starting over with a different tool.
